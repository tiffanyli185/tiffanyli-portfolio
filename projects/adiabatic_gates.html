Overview

A C2S2 side project redesigning a Flash comparator and 3-bit priority encoder using adiabatic logic instead of standard CMOS, aimed at cutting power consumption well below what a conventional design can achieve.

More Details

This project redesigned a Flash comparator and 3-bit priority encoder using adiabatic logic instead of standard CMOS. Adiabatic logic saves power by avoiding the abrupt voltage swings that cause standard CMOS to dissipate energy as heat every time a node charges or discharges. Instead, it uses a slow, ramping "trapezoidal" clock signal to charge and discharge nodes gradually, recovering most of that energy back into the power supply rather than losing it. The specific style I used is called SCRL (Split-level Charge Recovery Logic), which relies on multiple overlapping clock phases to control when each stage evaluates and holds its state, so energy gets recovered in steps rather than all at once. I designed and simulated everything in Cadence Virtuoso, from schematic capture through layout and post-extraction verification.

The layout stage was the hardest part. Because SCRL circuits are wired differently from standard CMOS, getting the top-level wiring to route cleanly took a lot of iteration, and I had to balance the width of the metal tracks against how large the transistors themselves were. After finishing the first layout pass, I found that shrinking the transistors actually reduced power further, so I resized them and had to redo the entire layout, then re-run testing to confirm the new version still worked correctly. On the design side, a big decision was how to generate the 16 clock phases the circuit needed. I built dedicated testbenches to verify the clock generation and worked out the correct spacing between phases and exactly when each trapezoidal ramp should begin, since getting that timing wrong would break the energy recovery the whole design depends on.

After the redesign, the power savings were bigger than I expected: about 7.2x lower than a double-tailed comparator, and 19–33x lower than a standard CMOS implementation, confirmed through post-extraction simulation that accounted for parasitic capacitance.

Having to redo the layout after resizing the transistors was a good lesson in how connected each step of this process really is. A change early on ripples into everything downstream. It pushed me to test more thoroughly at each stage instead of waiting until the end, and to get better at backtracing an issue to the step that actually caused it, rather than assuming it was whatever I'd touched most recently.
