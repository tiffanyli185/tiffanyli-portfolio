Overview

SensUs is an annual international student competition focused on designing biosensors for real medical applications. Each year, teams are given a target analyte — a molecule in blood or urine that needs to be detected — and have to build a complete sensing system around it.

SensTech is Cornell's competing team, and I lead the electrical subteam, which is responsible for everything from the sensing transducer interface to the power system and signal readout.

Goal

The goal was to build a portable, battery-powered platform that can accurately measure creatinine concentration in urine — a key biomarker for kidney function. The hardware needs to be compact enough for point-of-care use, which means aggressive constraints on PCB size and power consumption.

The sensor itself is an electrochemical biosensor, so the analog front-end has to perform precise current measurement (in the microamp range) while rejecting noise from the environment and the power supply.

What I did

I designed the analog front-end circuit — a transimpedance amplifier configuration that converts the sensor's current output to a readable voltage — and the power management system. I ran LTSpice simulations to verify noise performance and stability before laying out the PCB in KiCad.

Leading the electrical subteam also meant coordinating with the biochemistry and software teams to make sure the signal chain matched what the app expected, and troubleshooting hardware issues at competition.
