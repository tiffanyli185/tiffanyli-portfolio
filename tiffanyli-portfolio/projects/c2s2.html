Overview

Cornell Custom Silicon Systems (C2S2) is a student-run chip design team that tapes out real silicon on TSMC's 180nm CMOS process through multi-project wafer runs. As an electrical engineer on the analog/mixed-signal subteam, I'm responsible for designing and laying out analog circuit cells that get included in real fabricated chips.

This kind of work is pretty rare at the undergraduate level — most students don't get to work on actual tapeouts, so it's been a big part of why I got into chip design seriously.

Goal

The goal of the project is to design a functional analog subsystem — specifically a mixed-signal interface block — that meets strict area, power, and performance constraints for a shared silicon die. Every layout decision has direct consequences on silicon: routing parasitics, device matching, and latch-up rules all matter in ways simulation alone can't fully capture.

The comparator block I worked on needed to hit a target power envelope while maintaining the required switching speed — a classic analog tradeoff that required iterating between schematic and layout multiple times.

What I did

I did the full layout of several analog cells in Cadence Virtuoso — placing and routing transistors, running DRC and LVS clean, and coordinating with the digital team on interface timing. I ran SPICE simulations to verify performance before submitting to the tapeout flow.

Working inside a real tapeout schedule was a different kind of pressure than coursework — hard deadlines, shared design rules, and zero room for mistakes once the GDS goes to the fab.
